Phase-change actuators provide a compact approach for untethered soft robotics by converting thermal energy into
volumetric expansion of a working fluid. This study investigates a phase-change pouch actuator operating in a hybrid air-water environment, where thermal excitation and buoyancy jointly influence motion. A pouch filled with HFE-71DE was integrated into a lightweight exoskeletal limb that produces rotational bending at a hinge. Temperature response under different heating methods was examined, followed by evaluation of actuator geometries, energy consumption for vapor expansion, limb actuation, and submerged conditions. Experimental results show that the thick pouch actuator generated a larger volumetric expansion of 8.5 mm, compared with 5.8 mm for the flat design, while the flat actuator exhibited slightly higher actuation efficiency of 1.91 °/kJ compared with 1.88 °/kJ. Submerged experiments reveal that cold water suppresses phase change by acting as a thermal sink, whereas warm water provides convective heating and buoyancy assistance, enabling complete limb bending with an efficiency of 1.84 °/kJ. These results demonstrate the potential of environmentally assisted actuation for amphibious soft robotics.
