This study developed a soft actuator based on a flattube structure to support the sit-to-stand (STS) motion. Unlike conventional robotic or assistive devices that provide direct lifting forces, the proposed system aims to enhance motion stability by gently applying light-touch pressure around the pelvis to naturally facilitate muscle activation. The actuator is fabricated by introducing notches into a flat tube to form air pocket chambers that simultaneously induce inflation and bending when pressurized. By placing large air pockets only around the pelvic region and configuring the other sections as narrow air channels, the actuator provides localized compression precisely where it is needed to assist the initiation of the STS motion. The actuator  achieves a soft "light-touch" effect through both inflationinduced and bending-induced compression, simply by wrapping it around the waist and supplying air. The effects of air pocket geometry and width at the notch were evaluated as design parameters. It was found that larger air pocket areas produce greater bending angles, while a larger width at the notch also increases bendability but reduces compression force. The developed actuator was tested with three patients with Parkinson's disease, and the results showed that actuator assistance improved lower-limb joint extension during the STS motion. These findings highlight the developed actuator's potential as a core component for compact, soft, and effective assistive technologies that promote natural and user-friendly motion support.
